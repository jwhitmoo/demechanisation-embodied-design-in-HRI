# Materials

*Every design in this catalog is built from the same parts.*

---

## Parts

All parts are cardboard or tape. Nothing else. This constraint is deliberate — it keeps the toolkit accessible, cheap, and fast to iterate. If you cannot build a piece from what is listed here, redesign the piece rather than adding new materials.

### Cardboard

| Part | Description | Typical source |
|------|-------------|----------------|
| **Sheet — single-wall** | ~3 mm thick, easy to score and bend. Used for curved surfaces, lightweight panels, and flexible elements. | Cereal boxes, shoe boxes, packaging inserts |
| **Sheet — double-wall** | ~6 mm thick, rigid. Used for structural panels, enclosures, and load-bearing surfaces. | Large shipping boxes, appliance packaging |
| **Tube — narrow (Ø 2–3 cm)** | Used for handles, grips, finger extensions, and slim structural members. | Cling film rolls, aluminium foil cores |
| **Tube — wide (Ø 4–6 cm)** | Used for arm extensions, structural columns, and joint sleeves. | Postal tubes, kitchen roll cores, wrapping paper cores |
| **Tube — extra-wide (Ø 8–12 cm)** | Used for torso-fitting sleeves and large structural members. Cut from carpet tubes or rolled from double-wall sheet. | Carpet roll cores, or hand-rolled |

### Tape

| Part | Description | Use |
|------|-------------|-----|
| **Packing tape (50 mm, clear or brown)** | The primary joining material. Strong, fast, repositionable within the first few seconds. | Structural joints, laminating panels for stiffness, edge protection |
| **Masking tape (25 mm)** | Lower adhesion, easy to tear by hand. | Temporary holds during assembly, marking cut lines, comfort padding on skin-contact edges |

### Not included (and why)

| Material | Why excluded |
|----------|-------------|
| Glue (hot glue, PVA) | Requires a tool, adds drying time, creates permanent joints that resist iteration. |
| Fasteners (staples, brads, screws) | Require tools, create sharp points near the body, complicate disassembly. |
| Wire / string / elastic | Introduces a fundamentally different structural logic. The toolkit uses rigid members only. |
| Paint / ink / markers | The extensions are structural instruments, not costumes. Decoration distracts from the movement exploration. |

## Tools

| Tool | Purpose |
|------|---------|
| Craft knife or box cutter | Cutting cardboard to size. Retractable blade preferred for safety. |
| Metal ruler or straight edge | Guiding straight cuts and score lines. |
| Cutting mat or scrap cardboard | Protecting the work surface. |
| Scissors | Trimming tape, cutting curves, rounding edges. |
| Pencil or marker | Marking dimensions and cut lines. |

No power tools. No specialist equipment. If a workshop participant can find a knife and a ruler, they can build every design in this catalog.

---

## Preparing parts

Before starting a design, **pre-cut all parts to size**. Each design guide begins with a parts list showing the number, shape, and dimensions of every piece needed — lay them all out before you start taping, the way you would sort pieces before beginning a build. This makes the step-by-step assembly faster and reduces errors.

### Scoring and folding

To create a clean fold in cardboard, score the fold line first: place the ruler along the line and draw the back of the craft knife along it with light pressure — just enough to cut through the top layer of paper, not through the corrugation. Fold away from the scored side.

### Laminating for stiffness

Where extra rigidity is needed, laminate two sheets of single-wall cardboard together with strips of packing tape. Alternate the corrugation direction (one horizontal, one vertical) for maximum stiffness.

### Rounding tube ends

After cutting a tube to length, wrap the cut end with a strip of masking tape to soften the edge and prevent the corrugation from catching on clothing or skin.
