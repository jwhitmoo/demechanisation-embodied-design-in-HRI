# Combinations

Wearing extensions from different body regions together produces effects that are more than the sum of the individual pieces. Below are tested configurations with notes on their combined movement qualities.

---

## Pairing Guide

Not all combinations work equally well. Some conflict physically (two pieces that need the same hand, or that collide when the wearer moves). The configurations below have been tested and confirmed to be wearable, safe, and productive for movement exploration.

| Combination | Pieces | Physical compatibility |
|-------------|--------|----------------------|
| **Cuboid** | T1 (Shell) + H1 (Frame) | Good — the shell carries its own weight via shoulders; the frame sits above it |
| **Sentinel** | T3 (Slab) + H2 (Visor) | Good — both are hand-held, but on different axes (slab at torso, visor at face) |
| **Antenna** | T1 (Shell) + H3 (Crown) | Good — shell frees the hands to hold the crown's handle tubes |
| **Windmill** | T2 (Cage) + A1 (Reach) | Good — cage is shoulder-hung, reach extensions are forearm-mounted |
| **Semaphore** | T3 (Slab) + A2 (Paddle) | Requires a helper to hold the slab, or a shoulder-strap modification to T3 |
| **Locked** | T1 (Shell) + A3 (Bridge) | Good — shell is self-supporting, bridge is hand-held |

---

## Combination Notes

### Cuboid — T1 + H1

The wearer is enclosed in rigid rectangular volumes from shoulder to hip and around the head. Almost all flexibility is removed. Movement becomes whole-body: walking, rotating, and tilting are the only options. This is the closest a human body can come to being a cuboid. The movement quality is heavy, committed, and mechanical.

**Design bridge:** This combination is the embodied equivalent of the two-cuboid robot. Movement insights discovered here translate almost directly to servo parameters.

### Sentinel — T3 + H2

The slab presents a dominant front surface; the visor narrows the visual field forward. The wearer becomes intensely directional — a body that exists primarily along a single axis. Turning is rare and deliberate. The quality is watchful, alert, and focused.

**Design bridge:** Explores how a robot communicates vigilance and attention through orientation alone.

### Antenna — T1 + H3

The rigid torso shell prevents spinal compensation, so the crown's amplification effect is transmitted through the whole body. Every micro-sway at the head produces a visible response in the shoulders and hips. The quality is precarious, alive, and subtly oscillating.

**Design bridge:** Demonstrates how small movements at the extremity of a structure (the tip of the crown, or the top face of the upper cuboid) create a sense of aliveness.

### Windmill — T2 + A1

The cage extends the body's perceived volume; the reach extensions amplify arm gestures beyond the cage boundary. The wearer has an enormous kinetic envelope. Movements feel expansive and sweeping, but also risky — the extended arms can easily bump things, creating contact feedback through both the reach tubes and the cage tubes.

**Design bridge:** Explores the expressive range of large-arc movements versus small-arc movements on the same robot.

### Locked — T1 + A3

The shell prevents torso rotation; the bridge prevents independent arm movement. The wearer is doubly constrained — the only remaining expressive channels are leg movement, weight shift, and head rotation. This extreme constraint often produces the most inventive compensatory movements.

**Design bridge:** What happens when almost all degrees of freedom are removed? The answer is: the remaining ones become extraordinarily expressive. This is the core argument for the two-cuboid constraint.

## Creating Your Own Combinations

When combining pieces from different regions, check:

1. **Physical clearance** — do the pieces collide when the wearer moves normally?
2. **Hand availability** — do both pieces need the hands? If so, can one be modified with shoulder straps to free the hands?
3. **Weight distribution** — is the combined weight balanced, or does it pull the wearer in one direction? (An unbalanced combination is not necessarily bad — it may produce interesting compensatory movement.)
4. **Removal** — can the wearer remove all pieces within 10 seconds, unassisted?

Document new combinations by adding them to this file with the same format: name, pieces, compatibility notes, movement observations, and design bridge.
