# A3 — Bridge

> A rigid cardboard beam connecting both arms via tube handles, forcing them to move as a single linked unit and eliminating independent arm gestures.

**Body region:** arms
**Build time:** 10–15 minutes

---

## Parts required

Cut and lay out all parts before beginning assembly.

| Qty | Part | Material | Dimensions | Notes |
|-----|------|----------|------------|-------|
| 1 | Bridge beam | Wide tube (Ø 4–6 cm) | 80 cm long | Spans from hand to hand in front of the body |
| 2 | Handle tube | Narrow tube (Ø 2–3 cm) | 12 cm long | Inserted crosswise through the beam at each end — gripped by each hand |
| 2 | End cap | Double-wall cardboard | 8 × 8 cm | Covers the open ends of the beam tube |
| — | Packing tape | — | ~1 m | |
| — | Masking tape | — | ~0.5 m | |

## Assembly

### Step 1

> Take the **bridge beam**. Near each end (roughly 5 cm from the tip), cut two small opposing holes through the tube wall.

### Step 2

> Insert one **handle tube** through each pair of holes, creating a crosswise grip at each end. Tape in place. The handles should be perpendicular to the beam.

### Step 3

> Tape the two **end caps** over the open ends of the beam for a clean finish.

### Step 4

> Wrap masking tape around each handle tube for grip comfort. Tape over any rough edges on the beam.

---

## Fit and adjust

The wearer grips one handle in each hand, holding the beam horizontally in front of or across the body. The beam is rigid — moving one hand moves the other.

**Check:** The wearer can release both handles simultaneously to drop the beam. The beam should be light enough to hold at arm's length for several minutes without fatigue.

**Adjust:** Shorten the beam for a tighter coupling (arms closer together), lengthen for a wider span. If the beam is too heavy, switch to a narrower tube.

---

## Movement notes

The Bridge eliminates independent arm movement entirely. Raising one hand lowers the other. Reaching left pulls the right hand across the body. The wearer discovers a see-saw logic: every gesture has an equal and opposite reaction at the other end. This constraint produces rocking, tilting, and pivoting movements that feel mechanical and balanced — or deliberately unbalanced when the wearer fights the coupling. The see-saw quality often surprises wearers and produces movements they would never have invented from imagination alone.

---

## Design bridge

The Bridge makes visible the physics of coupled degrees of freedom — when one part of a system moves, another part responds. In the two-cuboid robot, the hinge joint between the upper and lower cuboids creates exactly this coupling: tilting the upper cuboid forward shifts weight backward in the lower cuboid. Servo A and servo D are mechanically independent but kinetically linked through the robot's centre of gravity. Wearers who experience the Bridge understand this coupling intuitively, and their prototyping reflects it — they tend to design movement sequences where A and D move in coordinated opposition.

---

## Variations

- **Vertical bridge:** Hold the beam vertically instead of horizontally. The coupling now links the hands in an up-down axis, producing lever-like movements.
- **Offset bridge:** Mount one handle 10 cm further in from the end than the other, so the pivot point is off-centre. The wearer's arms have unequal leverage.
- **Hinged bridge:** Cut the beam at the centre and reconnect with a tape hinge. The beam can now fold, introducing a second degree of freedom.
